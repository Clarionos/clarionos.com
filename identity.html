In person identification

In the physical world of face-to-face interactions, we think of identity as a face, maybe a voice, and in formal/stringent settings perhaps a driver’s license.

Verify yourself on your own

But in the digital world, we reduce all that richness to a few bits of personally-identifiable information. How does Twitter “know” you? They know you by your pieces of personally-identifiable information, typically, your username and password, maybe your phone number. That is how they know you.

Companies that can vouch for you

If you use your username to log into websites and apps, or if you use “Sign in with Google” or “Sign in with Facebook”, your ability to identify yourself to these websites is now in the hands of those entities that issued your account. What happens if that entity decides to deactivate your account? What happens if they deactivate your ability to login using your account? What happens when you go to update your email with these sites? Your email is commonly your primary form of identity and means of recovering a lost password. How can they verify it’s you?

When your data only exists with these providers, and they lock you out, you lose access to your emails, your photos… your data.

Self verification

If your identity and your data is independent of a single provider and redundantly stored, no company can interfere with your free activity on the web. If one avenue to your data goes down, there are other options.

Increase your options; expand your independence.
